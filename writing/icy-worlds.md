## Icy Worlds And Their Liquid Oceans

Enceladus, Saturn's second moon, orbits over 900 million miles from the Sun. The average temperature of its icy surface temperature is just -198 degrees Celsius. Yet scientists believe that below its surface is an ocean of liquid water.

In 2005, the Cassini mission relayed the first high-resolution image of Enceladus. Furthermore, the entire surface of the moon was conspicuously devoid of impact craters. This told scientists that the surface was very young: if meteor impacts have a certain probability of happening at any given time, then a crater-pocked surface must be older than a crater-free surface. In order to have a young surface though, scientists concluded that Enceladus needed some means of creating new surface and recycling old surface. On Earth, this is the role of plate tectonics. Was it possible that this obscure moon was tectonically active?

Then, scientists saw massive fractures running hundreds of kilometers along the moon's surface. They called them "tiger stripes." It was immediately noted that the tiger stripes were warmer than the rest of the surface. Soon, telescope observations discovered geysers of water and ammonia shooting into space out of the tiger stripes, and the mystery of Enceladus' young surface was solved.

The tiger stripes were cryovolcanos. Plumes of liquid water and ammonia erupted out of them at hundreds of kilometers per hour and settled on the surface, where they quickly settled and froze. This solved the mystery of Enceladus' young surface. Quickly after their creation impact craters were filled with frozen liquid water emitted from geothermal springs, essentially "filling the pot hole." However, in solving one mystery, scientists had created two more: where was this water coming from, and how was it possible that it could be liquid so far from the Sun?

There were two possibilities for the source of the water. Either it would be stored in small reservoirs scattered throughout the icy crust, or it could spring from a vast global ocean upon which the icy shell floated. 

To determine which of these was the case, Cassini was ordered to carry out detailed measurements of the moon's gravitational field. Solid ice and liquid water have different densities. This means that if the same size container is filled with both ice and water, each container will weigh different amounts despite being the same volume. Since more massive objects have stronger gravitational fields, by making detailed measurements of Enceladus' gravitational field, scientists could determine the relative proportion of ice to water in the interior of the planet. Combining this with information about the wobble Enceladus experienced in its orbit around Saturn (the same type of wobble responsible for giving the Earth seasons), scientists found that Enceladus must contain a global subsurface ocean averaging over 20 kilometers thick.

But of course, this begs the question: how is it possible that a moon orbiting millions of kilometers outside of the region warm enough for liquid water to exist on its surface could have such a vast ocean? 

Planets and moons can be heated by three processes. First is primordial heat of formation. The very early solar system was dense and hot. As planetary embryos began to accrete mass and grow into planets, denser materials would have sunk to the core of the embryo and lighter materials would have risen to the top. As this occurred, energy previously stored in the gravitationally unfavorable state with heavier materials at the surface and lighter materials at the core would be converted to heat. Over time, this heat is radiated out of the body's surface and into space. For a sufficiently large planet or moon, that heat could sustain liquid water for billions of years. In fact, this is one of the two dominant means by which the Earth is heated.

The other is radiogenic heating. Earth and many other bodies in the solar system are rich in elements such as uranium, potassium, and thorium. As these unstable isotopes decay, they emit radiation which heats their surroundings via the same mechanism used to generate heat in a nuclear reactor. In fact, the Cassini spacecraft itself used radiogenic heating to power its electronics. The bulk of Earth's heat is generated by the radioactive decay of uranium and potassium isotopes in its crust.

Neither one of these explanations were suitable for Enceladus though. Enceladus is small, only about the diameter of Arizona, and smaller bodies radiate their primordial heat much faster than large bodies. This meant that Enceladus' last joules of primordial heat were long ago radiated off into space. Radiogenic heating too was deemed to be an insufficient explanation. The quantities of unstable isotopes necessary to generate a subsurface ocean were too great to be compatible with estimates of the moon's composition based on its gravitational field.

This left only one possible heat source: tidal heating. On Earth, tides are generated by the Moon's gravitational pull. Because the Earth is a sphere, there is always a single point on the surface that's closer to the Moon than any other point. This means that the Moon will exert a stronger gravitational pull on that point than any other point on the Earth's surface. We experience high tides when that point or the point on Earth directly opposite it moves over us.

On Enceladus, strong tides are generated by regular close encounters with Saturn's next closest moon, Dione. These tides are far stronger than what we experience on Earth. They're strong enough, in fact, to cause Enceladus to stretch to an elliptical shape. This creates strong tidal forces with Saturn, and results in even more deformation. Ice sheets are pulled past each other and friction is created. This friction generates heat, which melts ice and keeps the subsurface ocean from freezing over.

In light of this mechanism, it turns out that subsurface liquid oceans are not unique to Enceladus. Europa, a moon of Jupiter, is warmed by similar tidal interactions with its host gas giant. This allows it to support an ocean that contains more than twice as much water as all of Earth's oceans combined.
